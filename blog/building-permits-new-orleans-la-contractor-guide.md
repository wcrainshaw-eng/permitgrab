## New Orleans's Building Permit System

New Orleans manages building permits through the **Department of Safety and Permits**, which operates the city's online permit portal and oversees all construction permitting. The system is designed to accommodate both straightforward projects and the complex historic preservation review process that characterizes much of New Orleans's construction activity.

### Where to Apply

**Online (Recommended):** New Orleans's permitting portal allows you to submit applications, upload plans, check status, and manage revisions electronically. Online submission is faster and provides clear documentation of all communications with the city.

**In-Person:** Visit the Department of Safety and Permits at **City Hall, 1300 Perdido Street, New Orleans, LA 70112**. Hours are Monday-Friday, 8 AM to 4:30 PM. In-person visits may be necessary for complex projects or to resolve plan review comments.

**Phone Support:** (504) 658-7100 for permit questions and status checks (Monday-Friday during business hours)

**Historic Properties:** Properties in the French Quarter (Vieux Carré) and other designated historic districts require additional approval from the **Vieux Carré Commission** after initial Department of Safety and Permits approval. This adds 2-4 weeks to the permitting timeline for these projects.

### Permit Costs in New Orleans

New Orleans's fee structure is designed to be accessible while funding the city's growing permitting infrastructure:

- **Residential permits (minor work/repairs):** $100-$600
- **Residential renovation/addition permits:** $800-$4,000
- **Commercial building permits:** $2,500-$12,000+ based on project valuation
- **Electrical, plumbing, mechanical permits:** $100-$500 each
- **Demolition permits:** $200-$800
- **Historic preservation review (additional):** $300-$1,500 depending on scope

**Key considerations:** Historic districts and preservation overlay zones may incur additional review fees. Some rehabilitation and adaptive reuse projects may qualify for expedited review or fee adjustments through the city's economic development incentive programs.

### New Orleans's Permit Timeline

New Orleans's permitting process follows a structured workflow, with timeline variations based on historic preservation requirements:

1. **Submission** — Online same-day or next business day in-person
2. **Pre-screening** — 2-5 business days for completeness check
3. **Plan review** — 10-20 business days residential, 15-30 commercial
4. **Historic preservation review (if required)** — 15-30 additional business days for Vieux Carré Commission approval
5. **Revision cycles** — If corrections needed, 5-10 business days per additional cycle
6. **Fee collection and issuance** — Upon approval, permits are issued after fees are paid
7. **Inspections** — Scheduled throughout the construction period

Projects with no plan review required (minor repairs) can often be issued within 2-5 business days.

## New Orleans's Construction Boom: Where the Growth Is

New Orleans's construction market spans historic preservation, hospitality, resilience projects, and neighborhood revitalization, each with distinct opportunities for contractors:

**French Quarter & Vieux Carré:** The historic heart of New Orleans generates steady renovation and restoration work. Historic building renovations, restaurant build-outs, hotel improvements, and boutique retail spaces drive commercial activity. The Vieux Carré Commission's strict preservation standards ensure that skilled restoration contractors are in constant demand. Properties are expensive, budgets for quality work are substantial, and historic preservation expertise commands premium rates.

**Warehouse District & Central Business District:** The conversion of 19th-century warehouses into apartments, lofts, galleries, offices, and creative spaces continues to accelerate. Mixed-use projects, industrial-to-residential conversions, and hospitality-related construction create demand for structural, MEP, interior finishes, and specialty trades. This area attracts developers with substantial capital and willingness to invest in quality construction.

**Bywater & Marigny (Arts District):** These gentrifying neighborhoods are experiencing significant renovation and new mixed-use development. Historic Creole cottages and shotgun houses are being restored and modernized. New restaurants, galleries, music venues, and creative businesses drive commercial construction. The neighborhood's bohemian character and increasing property values are attracting serious development capital.

**Mid-City & Uptown/Magazine Street:** These established residential neighborhoods continue to generate renovation activity as homeowners invest in historic properties and modern improvements. Adaptive reuse of commercial buildings into restaurants, offices, and retail spaces is ongoing. These neighborhoods offer steady, diversified project opportunities.

**New Orleans East:** Post-Katrina rebuilding continues with residential infill, commercial development, and infrastructure improvements. New construction and renovation projects here often qualify for economic development incentives and emerging contractor set-asides.

**Lower Garden District & Adjacent Historic Neighborhoods:** Victorian and historic architecture drives steady restoration and renovation demand. High-value properties and affluent homeowners create opportunities for quality contractors specializing in historic work and luxury finishes.

**Gentilly & Lakeview:** Ongoing post-Katrina recovery, elevation projects, and flood mitigation work combine with residential renovation to create diverse opportunities. Elevation and flood-resistant construction is a specialized market segment with steady demand.
