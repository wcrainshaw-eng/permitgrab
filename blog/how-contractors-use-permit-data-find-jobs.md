# How Smart Contractors Use Permit Data to Find Their Next Job

Most contractors have heard that building permits are public record. Fewer know how to actually turn that data into work. This guide walks you through the process from start to finish—no fluff, just the practical steps that working contractors use to find and win jobs from permit data.

## What Permit Data Actually Tells You

When someone files for a building permit, the public record typically includes the property address, the type of work being done (new construction, addition, renovation, demolition), an estimated project value, and the filing or issuance date. Depending on the jurisdiction, you may also get the property owner's name and the general contractor on record.

This is intelligence, not just data. Each permit represents a confirmed construction project with an approved scope and an estimated budget. Someone has already committed time and money to making this project happen.

## The Five-Minute Daily Routine

The contractors who get the most value from permit data don't spend hours on it. They spend five minutes every morning. Here's the routine:

**Step 1: Check your daily alerts.** Set up email alerts for your city and trade category. When new permits land in your inbox, scan them for relevant projects. A roofer looks for roof replacements and new construction. An electrician looks for commercial buildouts and residential additions. A general contractor looks at everything above a certain dollar threshold.

**Step 2: Identify the opportunity.** For each relevant permit, ask three questions: Is this project in my service area? Is it the type of work I want? Is the project size right for my crew?

**Step 3: Make contact.** How you reach out depends on what you find. If the permit lists a general contractor, they may need subcontractors. If it lists a property owner doing an owner-build, they almost certainly need help. If it's a commercial project, the GC is likely soliciting bids.

That's it. Five minutes of scanning, one or two targeted outreach efforts, and you've planted seeds that turn into jobs.

## Trade-Specific Strategies

### Roofers

Look for new construction permits (every new building needs a roof), re-roofing permits, and storm damage repair permits after major weather events. Also watch for large commercial permits—a new warehouse or retail building is a significant roofing contract.

The play: When you see a new residential subdivision being permitted, reach out to the builder early. Getting on their preferred subcontractor list means steady work, not just one job.

### HVAC Contractors

New construction and major renovation permits are your bread and butter. Any project over $50,000 likely involves HVAC work. Also watch for commercial tenant improvement permits—a new restaurant or office buildout always needs HVAC.

The play: Commercial permits with high estimated values often go out for competitive bid. Being first to know about the project gives you time to prepare a thorough proposal before the GC is overwhelmed with quotes.

### Electricians

Nearly every construction permit involves electrical work. Focus on commercial buildouts, restaurant permits (heavy electrical requirements), and residential additions. Solar installation permits are also a growing category in many markets.

The play: Monitor permits for new commercial spaces in strip malls and office parks. The tenant improvement phase is when electrical work gets specified, and many GCs haven't locked in their electrical sub yet.

### General Contractors

You have the broadest filter—look at everything above your minimum project size. New construction, major additions, full-gut renovations. Pay special attention to permits where no general contractor is listed yet, which suggests the owner hasn't hired one.

The play: When you see a large residential permit with no GC on record, that homeowner is either acting as their own GC (and may be overwhelmed) or is still shopping. Either way, a professional introduction is welcome.

### Plumbers

New construction, kitchen and bathroom remodel permits, and addition permits are your targets. Multi-family residential permits are especially valuable—a 20-unit apartment building is 20 kitchens and 40+ bathrooms.

The play: Build relationships with the GCs you see pulling permits repeatedly. If one builder is permitting 10 houses a quarter, becoming their go-to plumber is steadier than chasing one-off jobs.

## How to Reach Out Without Being Pushy

The biggest mistake contractors make with permit leads is treating them like cold calls. They're not. You have context that a cold caller doesn't—you know exactly what project they're working on, how much they're spending, and what stage they're at.

Use that context. Instead of "Hi, I'm a roofer, do you need roofing work?", try something like "I saw you recently permitted a new 3,200 sq ft home on Oak Street. I specialize in residential roofing in this area and would love to provide a competitive quote for the roofing package."

That's specific, relevant, and shows you've done your homework. It's the opposite of spam.

A few principles for permit-based outreach. Be specific about the project you're referencing. Mention a relevant credential or recent similar project. Keep it short—two or three sentences maximum for the initial contact. Follow up once, then move on if there's no response.

## Scaling Up: From One Job to a Pipeline

Once you've landed a few jobs from permit data, the real power becomes apparent: you can see market trends before they show up anywhere else.

If you notice permit filings for new construction accelerating in a specific neighborhood, you can position yourself there before the work boom hits. If commercial permits are trending up in your city, it's time to pursue your commercial license or certifications.

Permit data isn't just a lead source—it's market intelligence that helps you make better business decisions about where to focus, what services to add, and when to hire.
