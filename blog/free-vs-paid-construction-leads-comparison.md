# Free vs. Paid Construction Leads: What Actually Works in 2026

If you're a contractor looking for work, you've probably typed "how to get construction leads" into Google at least once. And you've seen the same advice recycled everywhere: buy leads from Angi, sign up for Thumbtack, build a website, ask for referrals.

But nobody talks about what these things actually cost—or whether they work. This guide breaks down every major lead source contractors use in 2026, with real numbers on cost-per-lead, close rates, and time investment.

## The Problem With Most Lead Generation Advice

Most "how to get leads" articles are written by marketing companies selling lead services. They'll tell you their platform is the answer, and every other option is inferior. The reality is more nuanced. Different lead sources work for different trades, markets, and business stages.

A solo roofer in a mid-size Texas city has completely different needs than a 15-person general contractor in Manhattan. Let's look at what actually works, starting with what's free.

## Free Lead Sources

### Referrals and Word of Mouth

This is still the highest-converting lead source in construction. Referral leads close at 50-70% because the trust is already established. The downside? You can't scale it on demand. When work dries up, you can't just "turn on" more referrals.

**Best for:** Established contractors with 3+ years in business.
**Cost per lead:** $0 (but years of relationship building).
**Close rate:** 50-70%.

### Google Business Profile

A well-optimized Google Business Profile (formerly Google My Business) puts you in front of people actively searching for contractors in your area. It's free to set up, and the leads that come through are high-intent—they're searching for "roofer near me" or "general contractor [city]" right now.

**Best for:** All contractors, especially those doing residential work.
**Cost per lead:** $0 (time investment for reviews and photos).
**Close rate:** 20-40%.

### Building Permit Data

This is the lead source most contractors don't know about. Every time someone pulls a building permit, that information becomes public record. A permit means a project is approved, funded, and moving forward. It's not a maybe—it's a confirmed construction job.

Monitoring new permits daily gives you a real-time feed of projects in your area before most of your competitors know about them. You can see the type of work, the estimated project value, and sometimes the property owner's name.

**Best for:** All contractors, especially those targeting commercial work or high-value residential projects.
**Cost per lead:** Free for basic alerts; Pro access for full details.
**Close rate:** 15-30% (higher than paid lists because these are real, approved projects).

### Social Media and Content Marketing

Posting project photos on Instagram, engaging in local Facebook groups, or sharing expertise on YouTube can generate inbound leads over time. The challenge is consistency—you need to post regularly for months before you see meaningful results.

**Best for:** Contractors comfortable with content creation, especially in visual trades (remodeling, landscaping, custom builds).
**Cost per lead:** $0 (significant time investment).
**Close rate:** 10-25%.

## Paid Lead Sources

### Angi (Formerly Angi Leads / HomeAdvisor)

Angi is the 800-pound gorilla of contractor lead generation. You pay per lead, typically $15-$80 depending on your trade and market. The leads are shared with multiple contractors (usually 3-4), which means you're racing to call first.

The biggest complaint from contractors? Lead quality. Many leads are tire-kickers, people who submitted a form out of curiosity, or homeowners who've already hired someone. The close rate reflects this.

**Cost per lead:** $15-$80.
**Close rate:** 5-15%.
**Annual spend for active use:** $3,000-$15,000.

### Thumbtack

Thumbtack works differently—you browse available jobs and choose which ones to quote. You pay when you send a quote, typically $10-$50 per quote sent. The upside is more control over which leads you pursue. The downside is heavy competition, especially in popular trades.

**Cost per lead:** $10-$50 per quote sent.
**Close rate:** 5-20%.
**Annual spend for active use:** $2,000-$10,000.

### Google Ads (Pay-Per-Click)

Running Google Ads for "roofing contractor [city]" or "kitchen remodeler near me" puts you at the top of search results. Clicks cost $10-$75+ depending on your trade and market. You need a good website to convert clicks into calls, and it requires ongoing optimization.

The upside is you control your budget and targeting precisely. The downside is it gets expensive fast, especially in competitive markets.

**Cost per lead:** $50-$250 (after accounting for clicks that don't convert).
**Close rate:** 10-25%.
**Annual spend for active use:** $6,000-$50,000+.

### ConstructConnect / Dodge

Enterprise-level project lead databases used primarily by commercial contractors and subcontractors. These platforms aggregate bid invitations, project plans, and construction pipeline data. Subscriptions start around $3,000-$6,000/year and go up significantly for full access.

**Cost per lead:** Varies (subscription model).
**Close rate:** 10-20% (for qualified bids).
**Annual spend:** $3,000-$12,000+.

## The Verdict: What Should You Actually Do?

There's no single best lead source—the smartest contractors stack multiple channels. Here's what we'd recommend based on where you are:

**If you're just starting out:** Google Business Profile + referrals + building permit alerts. All free, all effective. Get your GBP set up with photos and reviews, ask every happy customer for a referral, and sign up for daily permit alerts in your city.

**If you're growing and have budget:** Add Google Ads targeting your specific trade + city. Combine with permit data monitoring to catch projects that aren't advertised anywhere else.

**If you're established and want to scale:** Use permit data to identify market trends and target high-value projects proactively. A $500K commercial permit is worth a lot more than a $3,000 Angi lead for a faucet replacement.

**What we'd skip:** Buying generic lead lists from data brokers. The data is stale, shared widely, and the ROI is almost always negative.

## The Bottom Line

The contractors we see doing best in 2026 aren't relying on any single lead source. They combine the trust of referrals with the reach of digital marketing and the precision of permit data. The permit approach is especially powerful because you're finding real projects with real budgets—not competing to be the fastest caller on a shared lead.
